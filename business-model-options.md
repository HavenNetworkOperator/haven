# Business Model & Legal Structure Options for Haven

## The Core Tension

Haven needs to hold four things simultaneously:
1. **Community ownership** — the movement has skin in the game
2. **Aligned org benefits** — SFC and similar orgs share upside
3. **Investment readiness** — ability to raise capital when needed
4. **Founder financial comfort** — the founder is rewarded for building this

Most structures optimise for one or two of these. The art is finding the configuration that holds all four.

---

## Broad Sweep of Structures

### 1. Traditional Limited Company (Ltd) + Community Crowdfunding
**Example:** Monzo, Brewdog (early stages)

Standard Ltd, with equity crowdfunding rounds (Crowdcube/Seedrs) where community members and aligned orgs become shareholders.

- **Pros:** Simple, investment-ready, full flexibility
- **Cons:** Community "ownership" is real but diluted — shareholders have very limited voice; over many rounds, community stake shrinks
- **Haven fit:** This is what the current plan already points toward. It works, but it's ownership-lite.

---

### 2. Employee Ownership Trust (EOT)
**Example:** Waitrose/John Lewis, Richer Sounds

The company is owned by a trust on behalf of employees. No single founder retains controlling shares; the trust holds the majority.

- **Pros:** Strong tax advantages (0% CGT on sale to EOT), staff feel genuine ownership
- **Cons:** Designed for *employees*, not the broader community. Hard to use as a fundraising instrument. Not ideal pre-revenue.
- **Haven fit:** Low relevance early on. Could be a future destination structure once established.

---

### 3. Community Benefit Society (CBS) / Co-operative
**Example:** The Phone Co-op (literally a UK mobile co-op), Nationwide

Members own the organisation. One member, one vote. Surpluses distributed or reinvested per member benefit, not investor profit.

- **Pros:** Deeply mission-aligned; the *Phone Co-op* already exists as proof this works in telecoms
- **Cons:** Raising institutional investment is very hard — most VCs won't touch a co-op structure. Governance can slow decisions. Founder compensation has to be justifiable to members.
- **Haven fit:** Very on-brand. But if serious capital is ever needed, it boxes you in.

---

### 4. Benefit Corporation / B Corp (within an Ltd)
**Example:** Patagonia, Innocent Drinks (pre-Coca-Cola)

Standard Ltd, but with a legally embedded mission statement and accountability to stakeholders beyond shareholders. B Corp is a certification; "Benefit Corporation" is a legal status (not yet fully formalised in UK law, but Companies House allows mission-lock via articles).

- **Pros:** Keeps investment-readiness, signals mission credibility, prevents mission drift if you sell
- **Cons:** B Corp certification takes time and money. Doesn't by itself give community *ownership* — just commitment to purpose.
- **Haven fit:** Good signal layer. Should probably pursue regardless of the structural choice.

---

### 5. Steward Ownership / Purpose Foundation Model
**Example:** Ecosia (search engine), Zeiss (optics), Bosch

Two-class share structure:
- **Economic shares** — held by investors, founders, community (get dividends and value)
- **Golden share / purpose share** — held by a foundation or trust that has veto rights on mission-critical decisions

The "Purpose Foundation" model (pioneered in Germany, growing in UK) ensures no sale or mission drift can happen without the guardian foundation agreeing.

- **Pros:** You can raise VC/angel money via economic shares, AND lock mission permanently. Founder can get paid well. Community can hold economic shares.
- **Cons:** Complex to set up. Investors need to understand the structure. Limited UK case law.
- **Haven fit:** Probably the most interesting option. See recommended model below.

---

### 6. Hybrid: Ltd + Community Share Offer
**Example:** Octopus Energy (community energy model origins), many UK credit unions

Separate legal entity (a CBS or CIC) owns a meaningful stake in the main Ltd. Community members buy into the CBS. The CBS votes as a bloc shareholder in the Ltd.

- **Pros:** Clean separation — Ltd can attract normal investment; CBS gives community real voice; aligned orgs can join CBS
- **Cons:** Two entities to maintain. Community stake dilutes over investment rounds unless protected.
- **Haven fit:** Very workable. SFC and aligned orgs become members of the CBS. The CBS holds, say, 10–20% of Haven Ltd permanently.

---

### 7. Franchise / Licensed Partner Model

Not about ownership structure but worth noting — Smartphone Free Childhood and similar orgs become *licensed distribution partners* with revenue share, rather than equity holders. Simpler, but doesn't give them ownership.

---

## Recommended Model for Haven

**Steward ownership Ltd + community crowdfunding + aligned-org bloc**

Concretely:
- Haven is a UK Ltd with **two share classes**
  - **Ordinary shares** (founder, investors, crowdfunding backers) — economic rights
  - **Purpose share** (held by a small stewardship foundation — founder, SFC rep, independent trustee) — veto on mission-critical decisions: sale, pivot, changing the safety mandate
- **Crowdfunding** gives community members ordinary shares — they have economic upside
- **SFC and aligned orgs** get a small founding allocation of ordinary shares (or a seat on the foundation board), cementing the relationship and giving them real skin in the game
- **Founder** holds a significant ordinary share stake + takes a market salary — financially comfortable, and the stake has real value if Haven grows

**Why this works for Haven specifically:**
- The "by the movement, for the movement" story is *legally embedded*, not just marketing
- You can still raise from angels and VCs on standard terms (they hold ordinary shares like everyone else)
- No future acquirer can strip out the safety mission without foundation consent
- Community crowdfunding backers are genuinely co-owners — a powerful recruitment and retention story

## UK Structure Comparison

| Structure | Best for | Investment-ready? |
|---|---|---|
| Ltd (standard) | Most startups | Yes |
| CIC (Community Interest Company) | Social enterprise, asset-locked | Limited |
| CBS (Community Benefit Society) | Co-ops, mutual ownership | Hard |
| EOT (Employee Ownership Trust) | Post-revenue staff ownership | Not for fundraising |
| Charitable Incorporated Organisation | Pure charity | No commercial activity |
| Ltd + Purpose Foundation | Mission-lock + investment | Yes, with education |

---

## Key Principle

Haven's brand *is* the mission. The legal structure should reflect that — not as a constraint, but as a feature to sell to customers, investors, and movement partners alike. "We're legally incapable of removing the safety features to serve advertisers" is a stronger proposition than "we promise we won't."
