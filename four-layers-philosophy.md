# Editorial Stratigraphy

## Philosophy

A stratigraphic diagram rendered in the voice of a quiet broadsheet — an editorial cross-section where information accretes in horizontal layers, each one studied, measured, labelled, and left to speak for itself. The page behaves like a field-observation plate from a patient discipline: the kind of drawing that rewards slow reading and earns belief by showing its evidence rather than announcing its conclusions. Nothing is decorative. Every line is load-bearing.

The composition breathes on warm paper — the off-white tone of a good Sunday supplement, never the sterile brightness of a screen. Ink is the navy of a well-printed masthead: deep, steady, trustworthy. A single accent of terracotta earths the lowest stratum — the one that holds — and the eye travels to it naturally, the way it finds the fired clay under the surface of an archaeological drawing. Colour is restrained to three: the paper, the ink, the earth. Everything else is silence.

Rhythm is governed by horizontal bands of nearly equal weight, stacked vertically with generous air between them. Three of the bands carry visible breaches — rectangular apertures cut cleanly through the material, aligned as if a finger had walked through each layer and found the space where it could pass. The lowest band has no aperture. Its solidity is the whole argument. A hairline dashed trajectory descends through the apertures and terminates, with the restraint of a cartographer's tick mark, against the top edge of the solid stratum. The diagram says: here is where the path stops.

Typography is the work of craftsmen. A serif numeral stands beside each band — tall, quiet, authoritative, sized like a plate number on an engraving. Small capitals in a grotesque sans give the name of each layer and the one-line annotation that describes the breach. Monospaced microtype marks the figure number and the scale, used only where a technical hand would use it. Text appears where technical annotation would appear in a field diagram and nowhere else. Nothing floats. Nothing is decorative.

Craftsmanship is total. Every edge is measured to the pixel; every baseline aligns to the grid beneath the page; every gap between layers is the product of considered proportion rather than default spacing. The hatching inside each breach is drawn at a consistent diagonal, the stroke weight tuned against the outline weight so that both read at the same optical density. This is the work of someone who has spent years on plates like these — someone who knows that editorial weight is earned through the compound interest of a thousand small refinements, and that the final composition must feel as though it could not have been made another way.

The final object should read like a page torn from a patient discipline's reference atlas — the kind of diagram that survives on a wall for decades because it was made with the seriousness of master-level execution and the humility of someone who understood the subject well enough to explain it without raising their voice.
